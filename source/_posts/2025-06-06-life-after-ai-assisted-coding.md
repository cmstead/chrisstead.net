You are going to lose your job. This is the common refrain from people talking about generative AI and software development. There are a lot of folks who think software development is little more than pounding the keyboard. Even software developers have fallen prey to this idea. To those who work in software and believe writing code is the job, you are absolutely destined for the obsolescence. For the rest of you, read on!

Software Development is Cranking Out Code

This is a myth perpetuated by upper management who want to reduce software development to simply hammering keys. There is an innate desire to control every aspect of the process to mitigate risk. Of course, treating software development as mindless button-pushing introduces risk.

Software is an artifact of solving a problem. Writing code is the act of capturing a business process in excruciating detail. The real job of a software developer is solving a business problem. If someone were to hand you a file and say "this is a bunch of code", you would likely find it useless. When someone says "this is a word processor" the code has meaning.

Generative AI Will Take Your Job

This is a pipe dream. Generative AI doesn't understand business goals or business process. An LLM is trained with data and constructs combinations that are likely to be preferred by the user. If you want to do specialized training with the business context, great. You will have a business-specialized model. That business specialized model is only useful as a coding tool if it is also trained with source code. 

So here's the question, is your business a source code business?

It's highly likely your business is not a source code business. Your business relies on software to solve a business problem or meet a business goal. This means you are either going to want an LLM trained on your business context or an LLM trained on source code. One way or the other you are going to need a human to do the work of assembling solutions. You have two options. Option one is you will need a developer to query the model and build software from the learned context. Option two is you will need a developer to interpret shared business understanding and use the source code trained LLM to create software.

"AHA!" Your CEO says, "I will get TWO generative systems and they will talk to each other!"

Of course then you will need an AI specialist to train your models and a suite of developers to manage the interactions between the two models. Those same developers will need to take requirements from a human and feed them into this new frankensystem to generate software that is useful.

Your Job Will Look Different

Yes. Your job will look different. Things change when new technology becomes popular. You might not write as much of your code, but you will still need to read it. Martin Fowler type refactoring will become more important. You will not hand-author as many tests, but understanding good testing will become more important.

Something that already looks different to me is the speed of my feedback loop. I use Copilot to provide a first pass code review. I provide style and implementation examples and it comes back with suggestions. As a human, I get to choose whether I implement them or not, but I get first feedback faster than I would from the traditional pull request process.
